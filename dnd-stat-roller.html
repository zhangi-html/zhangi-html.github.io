Ability Score Roller

4d6 drop lowest · six stats · point-buy total of 35 or less, with leftover points to spend
  1. Roll 4d6, dropping the lowest, six times.
  2. Convert each roll to its point-buy value and sum all six. If the total is over 35, reroll the whole set.
  3. From there, use point buy (up to a budget of 35) to raise or lower your scores. Rules:
    • Scores can be modified from 6 to 18.
    • Bumping a stat up to 16 or higher requires an equal or greater number of stats at 8 or lower.
    • Bumping a stat up to 18 requires an equal or greater number of stats at 6 or lower.
    • At most 3 stats should be modified from their rolled values.
    • Rolled distributions can break the 16/8 and 18/6 balance; changing them through point buy can't.
    • Naturally rolled scores below 6 can be kept, and modified as desired, within the other existing requirements.
  4. Drag a stat card onto another to swap which roll fills which slot.
Thoughts

Goals, from the start:

  • Start with the standard array (15 14 13 12 10 8) and the alternate array (16 14 13 12 10 6), which is slightly stronger.
  • Add choice for randomness without introducing raw power.
    • I'm okay with "technically equivalently powerful" but otherwise unobtainable by the standard or alternate arrays.
  • You can choose the standard or alternate array with lineage ASIs instead of this system.
  • A level 1 PC should start with at most an 18.

Conclusions:

  • The standard array plus lineage ASIs (15 14 13 12 10 8) plus +2/+1 or +1/+1/+1 reaches +3/+3/+1 or +3/+2/+2 (equivalent to 35 point buy).
  • The ASI is built into the point buy, by balancing around the value after ASI application — that happens to also solve the problem of reaching higher than 18.
  • The alternate array reaches +4/+2/+2 or +3/+3/+2, but only at the cost of an extra -2 somewhere else in the array.
  • Modifying at most 3 stats mirrors the randomness and limited tweaking from rolling dice.
  • +3 should come at the cost of a -1, and +4 should come at the cost of a -2.
  • Rolling lets you have more +3s or +4s than normal — and if you're lucky, you could roll an 18 without needing a 6, or a 16 without needing any 8s.
